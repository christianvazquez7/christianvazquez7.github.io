Projecting written language to a musical plane


When subjected to certain channels of communication, such as written form, important information can be hidden in plain sight due to the excessive amount of data. Surely, if we could display information in such a way that the “noise” is abstracted, the task of a person analyzing the data would be more enjoyable. Take Fourier analysis as a signal-processing analog. When speech is analyzed in the realm of time, many properties of the signal are obscured, but when analyzed in the frequency domain, much superfluous information is abstracted, and a more human-understandable representation of the data is obtained. In the realm of written language, we often project meaning to an abstract plane. An example of this is the usage of literary figures such as metaphors, antithesis, or paradoxes that highlight specific features of the message that we want to transmit. The same could apply to many distinct scenarios, such as the projection of written text to a musical plane. There are many different elements in written text that could have, on a first look, a musical analogous. Rhythm can be linked to verbosity; tonality can be linked to topic. Another example would be, the correctness of the text could be linked to consonance or dissonance of the tune. The projection of written language to a musical plane can help us understand certain features of a long passage without even reading it. How long it is, the thematic behind the writing, the correctness,and even the particularities of the writer’s style. Artistically speaking, one could imagine generating music in real-time from the content of a public speech, or a mother reading a story to her child while a soft melody accompanies the story, bringing it to life!
